How a digital dhikr counter can deepen your daily remembrance

Nuria · Jul 16, 2026 · 4 min read

Why dhikr matters beyond the numbers

There is a reason the scholars of the past carried their prayer beads everywhere. Not to show piety, but to anchor themselves—to return, again and again, to the remembrance of Allah throughout the day.

الذكر—the remembrance of Allah—is one of the most consistently encouraged acts in the Quran and Sunnah. Allah says: "Remember Me, and I will remember you" (Al-Baqarah 2:152). It is not a complicated ritual. It does not require wudu. It costs nothing. And yet, of all the voluntary acts of worship, dhikr is perhaps the one most easily neglected in the noise of modern life.

The tasbih in a new form

For centuries, Muslims have used the tasbih—the 33-bead or 99-bead prayer beads—to count their adhkar. The physical act of moving a bead created something valuable: friction. A small, deliberate pause. A return to the present moment.

A digital dhikr counter does something similar. It replaces the bead with a tap, the string with a screen. This sounds like a diminishment, but it need not be. The tool is not the practice—your intention is.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Actions are by intentions, and every person will have what they intended." Whether you tap a screen or move a bead, if your heart is present with Allah, the remembrance is real.

Common dhikr to count

The most beloved adhkar are those the Prophet ﷺ mentioned specifically. Among the most well-known:

  • SubhanAllah—Glory be to Allah (33 times after prayer)
  • Alhamdulillah—All praise is for Allah (33 times after prayer)
  • Allahu Akbar—Allah is the Greatest (33 times after prayer, completing 99 with La ilaha illallah)
  • SubhanAllahi wa bihamdihi—Glory and praise be to Allah (100 times per day; the Prophet ﷺ said this erases sins like foam on the sea)
  • La ilaha illallah—There is no god but Allah

Each of these has a basis in hadith. None of them require a specific count to be valid as a form of worship—but setting a number creates accountability, and accountability creates habit.

Building a consistent practice

The greatest enemy of dhikr is not disbelief in its value—most Muslims know dhikr matters. The enemy is forgetfulness. We intend to sit after Fajr and say our adhkar, then the phone buzzes, the child calls, and the day has started before we began.

A few practical approaches:

  • Anchor it to salah. The adhkar after each prayer are well-established from the Sunnah. Before you put your phone down after praying, open the counter and complete them. This takes under two minutes for the post-salah adhkar.
  • Set a daily intention. At the start of each day, decide on one form of dhikr you will complete—even just 100 repetitions of SubhanAllahi wa bihamdihi. Write it down or set a gentle reminder.
  • Use waiting time. Commutes, queues, the minutes between meetings—these are not wasted time if your heart and tongue are in dhikr. A counter in your pocket removes the barrier of counting in your head.

Presence over performance

There is a real risk with any counting practice: it becomes mechanical. You hit 33, you move on. The number is done but the heart never arrived.

The scholars advise slowing down. Ibn Ata'illah al-Iskandari, in his Hikam, writes that a dhikr done with presence and understanding is worth more than thousands done with an absent heart. This does not mean counting is wrong—it means the count should serve presence, not replace it.

One practical method: after every ten repetitions, pause for a moment. Let the meaning of what you said settle. SubhanAllah—I am declaring the absolute perfection and transcendence of Allah. What does that mean for how I see my day? Then continue.

The cumulative effect

Dhikr is not primarily felt in the moment of counting. Its effect is cumulative. Regular remembrance of Allah reshapes how you see difficulty, how you respond to anger, how you greet the unexpected. It is described in a famous hadith as being like a person who remembers Allah among people who do not—"like a living person among the dead."

The digital counter is not the point. The presence of Allah in your awareness is the point. But for many of us, practical tools lower the barriers enough that the practice actually happens—and a practice that happens imperfectly is worth infinitely more than one planned perfectly and never begun.

A closing intention

If you are reading this and have not opened Nuria's dhikr counter today, consider this your gentle nudge. Open it, set a number—even just 33—and begin. Your Lord is listening. The remembrance you make in private, in the ordinary moments of your day, is not small. It is among the most beloved actions to Allah.

May Allah grant us hearts that remember Him in all conditions. آمين
