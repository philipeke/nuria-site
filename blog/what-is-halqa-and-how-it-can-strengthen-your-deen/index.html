What is Halqa and how it can strengthen your deen

Nuria · Jul 1, 2026 · 4 min read

There is something the Prophet ﷺ said that many of us have heard but perhaps not yet acted on: the most beloved deeds to Allah are those done consistently, even if small. Consistency is precisely where many of us struggle—not because we lack sincerity, but because we practice alone.

Halqa (حلقة) means a circle or ring in Arabic. In the Islamic tradition, a halqa refers to a gathering of people who sit together in a circle to study, remember Allah, or hold one another accountable in worship. Scholars would teach in halqas. Students of knowledge would form halqas to review what they had memorised. Communities would gather in halqas after Fajr. The concept is ancient, and its wisdom is plain: we do better when we are not alone.

How Halqa works in Nuria

Nuria brings this tradition into the everyday. With Halqa, you can form a small group with friends, family members, or fellow students—and set shared goals for your practice.

Here is how to get started:

  • Open Nuria and navigate to the Halqa section from the main menu.
  • Create a new Halqa and give it a name—it could be named after your city, your community, or simply "My Circle".
  • Invite people by sharing a link or code. There is no need for them to have a public profile.
  • Set a shared goal—for example, reading one juz of the Quran per week, or completing a set number of daily adhkar.
  • Each member logs their progress privately, and the group sees a collective view of how the circle is doing overall, without exposing individual details unless a member chooses to share.

The design is intentional. Accountability does not mean surveillance. Each person's specific progress remains theirs alone unless they choose to share it; what the group sees is a sense of whether everyone is on track, a gentle nudge to stay consistent.

Why accountability changes things

Researchers who study behaviour change consistently find that social accountability is one of the most effective mechanisms for sustaining new habits. But Islam did not need modern research to know this—the sunnah of praying in congregation, of gathering for knowledge, of reminding one another, is built on the same insight.

When you know that others in your circle are also making an effort today, something shifts. It is not pressure or judgment—it is solidarity. You are not the only one who sometimes finds Fajr difficult, or who has let their Quran reading lapse for a week. And knowing that someone else in your Halqa quietly pressed on is often enough to bring you back.

Suggestions for your first Halqa

Starting small is better than starting perfectly. A Halqa of two people is still a Halqa.

Some ideas to get you going:

  • Quran reading circle: Each member commits to reading a set portion each day. The collective target could be one complete Quran recitation per month shared across the group.
  • Dhikr accountability: A simple daily target—for example, completing the morning and evening adhkar—tracked together.
  • Knowledge circle: A weekly check-in where members share one thing they learned from a hadith, a book, or a khutbah.
  • Sunnah habits: Praying two extra rakaat daily, fasting Mondays and Thursdays, or giving small daily sadaqah.

You do not need to be at the same level of practice as the others in your circle. In fact, a mix of experience can be the most beneficial—those who are newer to a practice bring genuine enthusiasm, and those with more experience bring steadiness.

A note on intention

Whatever goal you set in your Halqa, the niyyah—the intention—matters more than the achievement. If you fall behind, do not disappear from the circle. Return with honesty. One of the most beautiful aspects of genuine Islamic community is that it holds space for human imperfection. The goal of Halqa is not to perform consistency, but to genuinely pursue it, together.

You can set up your first Halqa inside Nuria in a few minutes. Start small, keep the goal realistic, and let the circle do what circles have always done—bring people back to what matters.
