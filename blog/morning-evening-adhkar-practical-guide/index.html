The morning and evening adhkar: a practical guide

Nuria · Jul 17, 2026 · 4 min read

The morning and evening adhkar—the collections of supplications and remembrances that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ taught—are among the most accessible yet most transformative acts a Muslim can do each day. They take roughly ten to fifteen minutes, they require no special preparation, and they connect every ordinary morning and evening to something sacred.

This guide walks through what the adhkar are, why they matter, and how to make them a steady part of your day.

What are the morning and evening adhkar?

The adhkar (singular: dhikr, meaning "remembrance") are a set of phrases, Quranic verses, and supplications reported in sound hadith, which the Prophet ﷺ taught his companions to recite at specific times of day. The morning adhkar (adhkar al-sabah) are typically recited after the Fajr prayer until sunrise. The evening adhkar (adhkar al-masa') are recited after the Asr prayer until sunset.

These are not ritual obligations in the same category as the five daily prayers, but they are firmly established Sunnah—practices the Prophet ﷺ performed consistently and encouraged his companions to maintain.

Why the adhkar matter

There is a common misunderstanding that dhikr is only for those with free time—retired scholars, or people who have already mastered the "bigger" obligations. This misses the point entirely.

The adhkar are designed for people in the middle of life: the worker catching a bus, the parent managing a household, the student in exam season. The Prophet ﷺ did not teach these remembrances as extras for the spiritually advanced. He taught them as everyday armour—protection, presence, and gratitude woven into the texture of an ordinary day.

The Quran speaks to this directly: "Verily, in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest." (Ar-Ra'd, 28). The adhkar are one of the most direct paths to that rest.

What the morning adhkar include

While collections vary slightly across hadith compilations, the core morning adhkar typically include:

  • Ayat al-Kursi (Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:255)—recited once.
  • The last two verses of Surah Al-Baqarah (2:285–286)—recited once in the morning.
  • Surah Al-Ikhlas, Al-Falaq, and An-Nas—recited three times each.
  • The sayyid al-istighfar (the master supplication for forgiveness).
  • Supplications for protection, well-being, and gratitude for the new day.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever recites Ayat al-Kursi after every obligatory prayer, nothing prevents him from entering Paradise except death." (Al-Nasai, authenticated). This single dhikr, taking less than thirty seconds, carries that weight.

What the evening adhkar include

The evening adhkar follow a similar structure, with some variations:

  • Ayat al-Kursi—recited once.
  • Surah Al-Ikhlas, Al-Falaq, and An-Nas—three times each.
  • The dua for well-being: "O Allah, I ask You for pardon and well-being in this world and the next."
  • Supplications for forgiveness and protection from harm through the night.

Some scholars also include specific supplications for family, for sleep, and for seeking Allah's protection from trials.

How to build the habit

The single biggest obstacle is not forgetfulness—it is the feeling that you need to do all of the adhkar perfectly, in sequence, every time, or not at all. This all-or-nothing thinking causes most people to abandon the practice entirely after a few missed days.

A more sustainable approach:

  • Start with one dhikr. Ayat al-Kursi after Fajr is the natural first anchor. Do it for a week until it feels automatic.
  • Add gradually. Once one dhikr is settled, add another. The goal is consistency over completeness.
  • Attach it to prayer. The easiest trigger is the prayer itself. Immediately after making tasleem, before you move or check your phone, begin your adhkar.
  • Keep a dhikr counter. Traditional misbaha work well. Digital counters work too. The act of counting keeps you present.
  • Return without guilt. If you miss a day—or a week—return without self-recrimination. The Prophet ﷺ said: "The most beloved deeds to Allah are the most consistent, even if small." (Bukhari, Muslim). Missing a day and returning is itself an act of humility.

A final word on intention

Before beginning your adhkar each morning, take one breath and set your intention: you are doing this as worship, out of love for Allah, and in following the example of His Prophet ﷺ. That niyyah—that sincere intention—is what transforms a few minutes of recitation into something with lasting weight.

The morning adhkar are one of the most portable acts of worship in Islam. They travel with you into every kind of day—difficult, ordinary, joyful. They do not require a mosque, a specific dress, or a particular state. They only require presence and a few sincere minutes.
