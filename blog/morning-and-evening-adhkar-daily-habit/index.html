Morning and evening adhkar: building a daily habit

Nuria · Jul 19, 2026 · 4 min read

The adhkar—the remembrances of Allah—assigned to morning and evening are among the most consistent Sunnah practices a Muslim can build into their day. Unlike many acts of worship that require preparation or a specific location, they can be performed anywhere: seated after fajr, during a morning commute, or in the quiet moments before isha.

What are the morning and evening adhkar?

The morning adhkar are typically recited after fajr prayer, and the evening adhkar after asr—according to the majority of scholars—or after maghrib. They are drawn from authentic hadith found in collections such as Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Abu Dawud, and al-Tirmidhi. Among the most widely recited are:

  • Ayat al-Kursi (Quran 2:255)
  • Al-Ikhlas, Al-Falaq, and An-Nas—three times each
  • "Subhana Allahi wa bihamdihi"—100 times in the morning
  • The sayyid al-istighfar—the master supplication for seeking forgiveness
  • Various remembrances related to protection, gratitude, and tawakkul

Different scholarly traditions may emphasise different supplications within these sets, and the complete collection can take anywhere from ten to thirty minutes depending on your pace and depth of reflection.

Why consistency matters more than completeness

One of the most common struggles with adhkar is all-or-nothing thinking. Knowing there are twenty distinct supplications in the morning set and having only five minutes can lead to skipping entirely rather than doing a portion. This misses the spirit of the practice entirely.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "The most beloved deeds to Allah are those done consistently, even if they are small." (Bukhari, Muslim)

A shortened version recited daily will benefit you far more than the complete set done occasionally. If your mornings are rushed, begin with Ayat al-Kursi, the three Quls, and a sincere dua asking for protection and blessing. That is a real, complete practice—not a compromise.

Building the habit

Consistency in adhkar, like any act of worship, is helped by lowering the friction as much as possible. A few approaches that many Muslims find useful:

Attach it to an existing habit

Adhkar belong naturally after salah. If you already pray fajr, the morning adhkar slot is there—you simply need to remain seated for a few more minutes rather than standing immediately and moving on with your day.

Keep a counter

Some adhkar have specific counts: 33 times, 100 times. Using a physical tasbih or a digital dhikr counter helps maintain focus without tracking a number in your head while simultaneously trying to concentrate on meaning and presence.

Start with what you already know by heart

Memorisation is not a prerequisite. Reading from a book or a screen is perfectly fine. But those phrases already committed to memory—Ayat al-Kursi, the three Quls—will become your anchor in moments when a screen is not at hand. Over months, more phrases will join that memorised core naturally.

Be patient with yourself

There will be mornings when fajr leads directly into the demands of the day and the adhkar simply do not happen. Missing a session is not a spiritual failure. The intention and the willingness to return is itself an act of the heart—and tomorrow is another chance.

The evening as a second chance

One of the mercies embedded in this practice is that there are two sets—not one. The evening adhkar are not simply a repeat of the morning; they contain their own distinct supplications with meanings related to seeking forgiveness for the shortcomings of the day, placing oneself under Allah's protection through the night, and renewing gratitude before sleep.

If mornings rarely allow for the full practice, the evening session—in the quiet after asr or after maghrib—often offers more space. For many people, the evening becomes the more sustainable habit anchor, and the morning becomes the secondary practice they build toward over time.

What this practice does to a day

Those who build this habit describe a shift not in external circumstances but in how the day feels. Not a vague sense of calm, but something more specific: a different quality of attention when difficulties arise, a natural return to remembrance in scattered moments throughout the day, and a sense of bookending each day with meaning and intentionality.

The adhkar do not make problems disappear. They offer a framework—a beginning and an end—within which everything else happens as amanah: a trust held from Allah, not simply a set of tasks to manage or endure. Over time, that shift in orientation is the real gift of the practice.
