Building a daily dhikr practice that lasts

Nuria · Jul 21, 2026 · 5 min read

The Prophet ﷺ said: "The best deeds are those which are done consistently, even if they are small." (Bukhari, Muslim)

Most of us have experienced the feeling—a surge of spiritual energy after Ramadan, a retreat, or a moving khutbah, followed by a slow drift back into the busyness of daily life. Dhikr—the remembrance of Allah—is one of the most powerful antidotes to that drift. But building a practice that lasts requires more than good intentions.

What is dhikr, and why does it matter?

Dhikr literally means "remembrance" or "mention." In Islamic practice, it refers to any act of remembering Allah—whether through reciting SubhanAllah, Alhamdulillah, and Allahu Akbar after each prayer; repeating la ilaha illallah throughout the day; or sitting quietly with your tasbeeh, heart turned toward your Lord.

Allah says in the Quran: "Verily, in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest." (Ar-Ra'd, 13:28)

This is not a metaphor. It is a promise. And it is a promise that holds regardless of your circumstances—whether you are in a moment of ease or difficulty, in a city or a quiet room, young or old.

The scholars of Islam—across all four madhhabs—agree on the immense virtue of dhikr. It is one of the few acts of worship with almost no barrier to entry: no wudu required for most forms, no specific time, no minimum quantity. It meets you wherever you are.

Why consistency matters more than quantity

Many people approach dhikr with an all-or-nothing mindset: "I'll do a thousand SubhanAllahs today," followed by forgetting about it entirely for a week. This is understandable, but it is not the prophetic way.

The Prophet ﷺ himself maintained consistency in his worship even when ill or travelling—a shorter prayer, but uninterrupted. His character was that of a person who preferred small, steady acts over large, sporadic ones. Scholars explain this is because consistent worship trains the heart over time, slowly drawing it closer to Allah in a way that bursts of effort cannot sustain.

Think of it less like a sprint and more like tending a garden. You water it a little every day, and over time, something grows that would never emerge from a single hour of intense effort.

Practical ways to build your dhikr habit

Attach it to what you already do

The most reliable way to build any new habit is to link it to something you already do reliably. The morning cup of tea. The walk from the car to the office. The moment before you open your phone in the morning.

Choose one such anchor and commit to pairing it with a specific dhikr. Even ten repetitions of Astaghfirullah while the kettle boils is a beginning. That beginning, protected and repeated, becomes a practice.

Start with the prophetically recommended sets

The Prophet ﷺ specified certain morning and evening adhkar—collections of remembrances to be said at particular times. These include Ayat al-Kursi, the last two verses of Surah al-Baqarah, and the tasbih of SubhanAllah, Alhamdulillah, and Allahu Akbar (33 times each after each prayer).

These prophetically grounded sets are an excellent starting point. They are short enough to complete in under ten minutes, yet scholars describe their spiritual weight as immense. Start there before adding anything else.

Use a counter—physical or digital

There is wisdom in having something in your hand. A physical tasbeeh forces a gentle pause in the body, a small, repeated gesture that anchors the mind. The tool is not the point. Presence is the point. But a good tool can serve that presence.

Involve your tongue and your heart together

It is possible to count a hundred SubhanAllahs while thinking about dinner. Scholars note that dhikr with the tongue alone carries some reward, but dhikr that reaches the heart—where you are genuinely turning your attention to Allah as you speak—is on a different level entirely.

This is not a reason to stop when your mind wanders. It is a reason to gently return. Each time you notice your mind has drifted and you bring it back, that act of returning is itself a form of remembrance.

What to expect—and what not to expect

A consistent dhikr practice does not usually produce dramatic spiritual breakthroughs in the first week. What it produces, over months and years, is a gradual softening of the heart. A greater ease in returning to prayer. A quieter interior life. A sense, when things are difficult, that you are not facing them alone.

Ibn Ata'illah al-Iskandari, the great scholar and spiritual teacher, wrote: "Do not abandon the dhikr because your heart is not present in it. For your forgetting of Allah entirely would be worse than your dhikr with heedlessness."

This is one of the most comforting and realistic pieces of guidance in the Islamic tradition. You do not have to be perfect. You have to be present—imperfectly, honestly, consistently.

A beginning, not a destination

Start today. Choose one dhikr. Attach it to one moment in your day. Do not wait until you have the right beads, the right app, the right mood. The remembrance of Allah is available to you right now, in whatever state you are in.

Over time, that single thread of remembrance can become something that holds your whole day together—a quiet thread of connection running beneath the noise of everything else.

May Allah make our tongues moist with His remembrance, and may He place His remembrance in our hearts—not just on our lips.
