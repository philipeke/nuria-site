Muharram: a month of renewal and intention

Nuria · Jun 29, 2026 · 4 min read

The Islamic new year begins quietly—no fireworks, no countdowns, no grand celebrations. Instead, Muharram arrives with something more enduring: an invitation to reflect, to renew, and to return to Allah with a sincere heart.

Muharram is the first month of the Hijri calendar, and it holds a distinction that sets it apart from the months that follow. Allah describes it as one of the four sacred months:

"Indeed, the number of months with Allah is twelve months in the record of Allah from the day He created the heavens and earth; of these, four are sacred." (At-Tawbah, 9:36)

The four sacred months—Dhul Qa'dah, Dhul Hijjah, Muharram, and Rajab—are set apart not to burden us, but to offer an extraordinary opportunity. In these months, the reward for good deeds is multiplied. Likewise, negligence carries greater weight. This is not meant to frighten, but to heighten our awareness: when something carries more weight, we give it more of ourselves.

Why Muharram is called "the month of Allah"

In a hadith narrated by Abu Hurayrah (may Allah be pleased with him), the Prophet ﷺ said:

"The best of fasting after Ramadan is the month of Allah—Muharram. And the best of prayer after the obligatory prayer is the night prayer." (Muslim)

The phrase "the month of Allah" carries profound meaning. Of all twelve months, this is the one the Prophet ﷺ attributed directly to Allah—a title that holds honour and intimacy. It is as if this month belongs to Him in a special way, and in honouring it, we draw closer to Him.

The Day of Ashura: its significance and its fast

Within Muharram, the tenth day—known as Yawm Ashura—holds a particular place. On this day, Musa (peace be upon him) and the Children of Israel were saved from Pharaoh, and Musa fasted in gratitude to Allah. When the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Madinah and learned that the people of the Book fasted on this day, he said:

"We are more deserving of Musa than you."

He ﷺ then fasted on that day and encouraged the companions to do so. When asked about the fast of Ashura, he ﷺ said:

"It expiates the sins of the previous year." (Muslim)

This is a profound gift—one day of fasting, and a year's worth of minor sins are forgiven. The scholars also recommended fasting the ninth of Muharram alongside the tenth, in order to differ from those who fasted only the tenth. The fast is a sunnah—a voluntary act of gratitude, not an obligation.

A time for honest intention-setting

Beyond the fast, Muharram is a time for genuine self-examination. A new year in the Hijri calendar is not merely a change of number—it is a prompt. What did the year behind us teach us about our relationship with Allah? Where did we fall short in our prayers, neglect those we love, or let the noise of dunya crowd out the quiet of remembrance?

The concept of niyyah—intention—is foundational in Islam. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Actions are judged by their intentions." Muharram is a fitting moment to sit with this teaching—not with guilt, but with honesty. What do we want the coming year to look like in the sight of Allah?

Some practical ways to honour Muharram:

  • Fast on the ninth and tenth of Muharram (Ashura), or the tenth and eleventh.
  • Increase voluntary dhikr—especially SubhanAllah, Alhamdulillah, Allahu Akbar, and Astaghfirullah.
  • Set one or two intentions for the year: a habit to build, a relationship to mend, a Quran goal to reach.
  • Spend time in sincere dua, asking Allah to make the coming year one of growth, steadiness, and closeness to Him.

Remembrance in the sacred months

The sacred months are not meant to feel heavy. They are gifts—concentrated windows of barakah where ordinary routines can become acts of worship, and where returning to Allah is made a little easier. This is the nature of His mercy: He does not leave us without signposts.

One of the reasons Nuria was built is to support exactly these moments—not to replace personal reflection, but to accompany it. Whether you use the dhikr counter on Ashura morning, read a few verses of the Quran with the word-by-word audio, or set a daily reminder for Fajr, each small act done with intention is a step toward Him.
