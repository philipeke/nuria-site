How to perform Istikhara: seeking guidance through prayer

Nuria · Jul 24, 2026 · 4 min read

Istikhara—seeking the good from Allah in decision-making—is one of the most misunderstood acts of worship in everyday Muslim life. Many Muslims have heard of it; fewer feel confident performing it. This guide walks through the prayer, the dua, and the wisdom behind it, without overstating what Istikhara promises.

What is Istikhara?

The word "istikhara" (الاستخارة) comes from the Arabic root خ-ي-ر, meaning "to seek the good." When you perform Istikhara, you are asking Allah to guide your choice and to make the good outcome easy for you—and to turn you away from what would harm you, even if you currently desire it.

The Prophet ﷺ taught his companions Istikhara for every matter in the same way he taught them a surah from the Quran, indicating how central it is to a believer's life. (Reported by al-Bukhari)

When to perform it

Istikhara is appropriate for any decision where both options are permissible (halal) and you are genuinely uncertain. Common examples include:

  • Marriage decisions
  • Career moves or job offers
  • Business partnerships
  • Travel plans
  • Major purchases

It is not performed for decisions that are already obligatory—you do not make Istikhara about whether to pray. Nor is it appropriate for things that are clearly forbidden, or for trivial everyday choices.

How to perform it

Istikhara consists of two rak'ahs of voluntary prayer followed by the specific dua. Here is how to approach it:

  • Make a sincere intention (niyyah) to pray two rak'ahs for Istikhara, separate from the obligatory prayers.
  • In the first rak'ah after Surah al-Fatihah, many scholars recommend Surah al-Kafirun; in the second, Surah al-Ikhlas—though reciting any surah is valid.
  • After completing the taslim, recite the Istikhara dua while holding the matter in your heart. You do not need to name it aloud—Allah already knows.

The Istikhara dua

The dua was reported by Jabir ibn Abdullah (رضي الله عنه). In transliteration:

Allahumma inni astakhiruka bi-'ilmika wa astaqdiruka bi-qudratika wa as'aluka min fadlikal-'azim. Fa-innaka taqdiru wa la aqdir, wa ta'lamu wa la a'lam, wa anta 'allamu-l-ghuyub.

Allahumma in kunta ta'lamu anna hadhal-amra—[hold the matter in your mind here]—khayrun li fi dini wa ma'ashi wa 'aqibati amri, fa-qdurhu li wa yassirhu li thumma barik li fihi. Wa in kunta ta'lamu annahu sharrun li fi dini wa ma'ashi wa 'aqibati amri, fasrifhu anni wasrifni anhu, waqdur liyal-khayr haythu kana thumma ardini bihi.

The meaning: "O Allah, I seek Your guidance through Your knowledge, and I seek capability through Your power, and I ask of Your great bounty. For You are capable and I am not, You know and I do not, and You know the unseen. O Allah, if You know that this matter is good for me in my religion, my livelihood, and the outcome of my affairs—ordain it for me and make it easy, and bless me in it. And if You know that it is harmful to me in my religion, my livelihood, and the outcome of my affairs—then turn it away from me and turn me away from it, and ordain for me the good wherever it may be, then make me content with it."

Common misconceptions

Many Muslims wait for a vivid dream—often imagined as green or white light—before acting. Scholars clarify that this expectation has no basis in the hadith. The response to Istikhara can be as simple as a door opening or closing, a feeling of ease or unease, or the natural unfolding of events after you have placed your trust in Allah.

Some people repeat Istikhara seven times. This practice has no established basis in the Sunnah. Performing it once, with sincerity, is sufficient. If you remain uncertain after some days, you may repeat it—but the goal is not to keep asking until you receive the answer you already want.

Istikhara is also not a substitute for research, consultation (shura), and careful planning. The Prophet ﷺ was known for both thorough deliberation and complete trust in Allah—these two things are meant to complement each other, not compete.

After you decide

Once you have made your decision and taken practical steps forward, put your trust fully in Allah (tawakkul). Whatever the outcome—whether it appears in your eyes as ease or as difficulty—is the khair that Allah has decreed for you. A path that seems hard at first may carry the greatest good in the long run.

This is perhaps the deepest reassurance that Istikhara offers: you are not navigating life alone. No sincere decision made with prayer and honest intention is ever wasted.
