How to make dua with focus and sincerity

Nuria · Jul 6, 2026 · 4 min read

Dua—supplication—sits at the heart of the relationship between a believer and Allah. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: "Dua is worship itself." (Abu Dawud) Yet many of us have experienced standing in prayer, lips moving through words we have memorised, while our minds wander elsewhere. We finish the dua, and it feels hollow.

This is not failure. It is a starting point.

What makes a dua accepted?

Scholars across the madhhabs agree that Allah hears every dua. The question of acceptance belongs entirely to Him, and His ways are beyond our full understanding. What we can attend to is the quality of our presence—our khushu' in supplication.

The Quran tells us:

"Call upon your Lord in humility and privately. Indeed, He does not like transgressors." (Al-A'raf, 55)

Two words carry the meaning: humility (tadorru') and privacy (khifyah). Together they point to a dua made with a lowered heart, not a performance for others.

Start with stillness

Before words, there needs to be a moment of stillness. This is practical, not mystical. When you stop what you were doing, take a breath, and sit for a few seconds in quiet awareness of where you are and who you are speaking to—you are not wasting time. You are preparing the ground.

The Prophet ﷺ recommended beginning dua with praise of Allah (hamd) and salawat upon the Prophet, then bringing your need. This structure naturally slows you down. You cannot rush through hamd and salawat. The form itself invites presence.

Use Arabic—and understand it

Many practising Muslims have memorised duas in Arabic without knowing their full meaning. This is a starting point, but the heart can only be moved by what the mind understands. If you have been reciting Rabbana atina fid-dunya hasanatan... for years, spend twenty minutes reading a translation and tafsir of those few lines. The next time you recite them, you will feel the difference.

Make it personal

The duas preserved in the Sunnah are a treasury. But the Prophet ﷺ also instructed his companions to ask Allah for their specific needs—in their own words, in their own language. You are allowed to speak to Allah directly. You do not need to find the perfect text.

Tell Allah what is in your heart, even the things that feel too small to mention. A worry about your child. A fear about your work. The tiredness that has settled in your body. Nothing is too small for the One who holds the weight of the entire universe.

Choose your times with care

Certain times carry particular weight for dua:

  • The last third of the night: Allah descends—in a manner befitting His majesty—and asks: "Who is calling upon Me, that I may answer him?" (Bukhari and Muslim)
  • Between the adhan and iqama: A brief window, easy to miss if you are not attentive
  • The last moments of Friday: Scholars differ on the exact time, but attentiveness throughout the day keeps the heart open
  • While prostrating (sujud): "The servant is closest to his Lord while prostrating." (Muslim)
  • After the obligatory prayers: A time many of us rush past

You do not need to use every window. One consistent time, kept every day, is worth more than many scattered intentions.

On not seeing an immediate answer

It is human to feel discouraged when a dua seems unanswered. The Prophet ﷺ warned us against this:

"The dua of any of you will be answered as long as he does not become impatient and say: 'I made dua and it was not answered.'" (Bukhari and Muslim)

The scholars explain three possible outcomes for every sincere dua: it is granted as requested; it is stored as a blessing for the hereafter; or it averts some harm that was otherwise decreed. In all three cases, the dua was not wasted. This understanding—held as a genuine conviction, not just a consolation—changes the experience of waiting.

Building a practice, not a ritual

The goal is not to check off a dua from a list each day. The goal is to become someone who is in ongoing conversation with their Lord—someone for whom turning to Allah is as natural as breathing.

This takes time. It takes failure and starting again. It takes days when the words come easily and days when they do not.

Begin where you are. One dua, one time, with as much presence as you can bring today. Tomorrow, the same. Over weeks and months, you will notice something shift—not in the circumstances of your life, necessarily, but in the quality of your relationship with the One you are speaking to.

That is the real gift of dua.
