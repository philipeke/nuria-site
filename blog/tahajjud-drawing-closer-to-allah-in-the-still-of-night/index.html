Tahajjud: drawing closer to Allah in the still of night

Nuria · Jul 14, 2026 · 5 min read

A prayer the world doesn't see

There is a particular quality to the hours before Fajr. The streets are quiet, the household is still, and the world—for a moment—feels held in a kind of sacred pause. It is in this stillness that Tahajjud, the night prayer, finds its home.

Tahajjud is a voluntary prayer (nafl) performed after Isha and before Fajr, ideally in the last third of the night. It is not obligatory, but it is among the most consistently praised acts of worship in the Quran and Sunnah. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ prayed it throughout his life, and urged his companions to do the same—not out of obligation, but out of a deep understanding of what it offers.

What the Quran says about the night prayer

Allah speaks of the night prayer in Surah Al-Muzzammil (73:1–4): "O you who wraps himself [in clothing], arise [to pray] the night, except for a little—half of it—or subtract from it a little, or add to it, and recite the Quran with measured recitation."

Later in the same surah, Allah acknowledges the wisdom: "Indeed, the hours of the night are more effective for concurrence [of heart and tongue] and more suitable for words." (73:6) The night is described not as a burden but as a gift—a window of clarity when the heart and tongue align more naturally.

In Surah Al-Isra, Allah tells the Prophet ﷺ: "And from [part of] the night, pray with it as additional [worship] for you; it is expected that your Lord will resurrect you to a praised station." (17:79) This verse has led scholars across all four madhhabs to consider Tahajjud among the most virtuous voluntary acts a Muslim can perform.

How to pray Tahajjud

Tahajjud is prayed in units of two rakats at a time. You may pray as few as two rakats or continue for as long as you are able—the Prophet ﷺ himself sometimes prayed eight rakats or more, followed by the Witr prayer.

The method is the same as any other prayer:

  • Make your intention (niyyah) for Tahajjud
  • Begin with two rakats, reciting whatever you are able from the Quran
  • Offer the Witr prayer at the end if you have not already prayed it after Isha
  • Close with dua—this is one of the most powerful times to ask of Allah

Across the four madhhabs, the general ruling is consistent: Tahajjud is a confirmed sunnah (sunnah mu'akkadah) that the Prophet ﷺ never abandoned. There are minor differences in detail—such as whether to pray Witr before sleeping and repeat it after rising—and following your own madhhab on these points is entirely appropriate.

The last third of the night

A hadith recorded in both Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim describes what happens in that final portion of the night: "Our Lord descends to the lowest heaven during the last third of the night, and He says: 'Who is calling upon Me, that I may answer him? Who is asking from Me, that I may give him? Who is seeking My forgiveness, that I may forgive him?'"

This is one of the most moving descriptions in all of Islamic tradition. It is not asking for perfection—it is asking for presence. If you find yourself awake at 3am, unable to sleep, consider that perhaps this is why.

Starting small, staying honest

There is a tendency, when we first encounter Tahajjud, to set ambitious goals: every night, long rakats, extended dua. This almost always leads to inconsistency. The Prophet ﷺ advised something different: "The most beloved deeds to Allah are those done consistently, even if they are small."

A more sustainable beginning might look like this:

  • Choose one or two nights a week—Friday night (the night before Jumu'ah) is especially recommended
  • Set an alarm for 30–45 minutes before Fajr
  • Pray just two rakats to start—and sit for a few minutes in dua
  • Do not judge the experience. Some nights will feel full of light; others will feel dry. Both are part of the path.

What it gives back

Those who pray Tahajjud consistently describe something that is hard to put into words: a sense that the rest of the day is different. Not easier, necessarily, but more grounded. The Prophet ﷺ said: "You should pray Qiyam al-Layl (the night prayer), for it was the practice of the righteous who came before you. It is a means of closeness to Allah, an expiation for sins, a deterrent from wrongdoing, and a protection from disease." (Al-Tirmidhi)

This is not a promise that life becomes effortless. It is a promise of something quieter and more durable: a thread of connection that, with time and sincerity, becomes one of the most precious parts of your deen.
